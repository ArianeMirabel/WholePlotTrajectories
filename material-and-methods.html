Post-Disturbance Diversity Trajectories in a Neotropical Forest

2 Material and Methods

2.0.1 Study site

Paracou station in French Guiana (5°18’N and 52°53’W) is located in a lowland tropical rain forest corresponding to a tropical wet climate with mean annual precipitation averaging 2980 mm.y-1 (30-y period) with a 3-months dry season (< 100 mm.months-1) from mid-August to mid-November, and a one-month dry season in March Wagner et al. (2011). Elevation ranges between 5 and 50 m and mean annual temperature is 26°C. Soils correspond to thin acrisols over a layer of transformed saprolite with low permeability generating lateral drainage during heavy rains. The experiment corresponds to a network of twelve 6.25ha plots where three logging, thinning and fuelwood treatments on an intensity gradient. Disturbance treatments were attributed according to a randomized plot design with three replicate blocks of four plots. The logging correspond to averages of 10 trees removed per hectare with a diameter at 1.3 m height (DBH) above 50 cm for treatment 1 (T1), 32 trees/ha above 40 cm DBH for treatment 2 (T2) and 40 trees above 40 cm DBH for treatment 3 (T3). Treatments T2 and T3 included besides logging of 10trees/ha the thinning by poison girdling .

2.0.2 Inventories protocol and dataset correction

The study site corresponds to a tropical rain forest with a dominance of Fabaceae, Chrysobalanaceae, Lecythidaceae and Sapotaceae. In the twelve experimental plots of the experiment, all trees above 10 cm DBH are mapped and measured annually since 1984. During the inventories, trees are first identified with a vernacular name, assigned by the field team, and then with a scientific name, assigned by a botanist during regular botanical campaigns. In 1984, specific vernacular names were given to 62 commercial or common species whereas two identifiers separating palm trees and trees were given to all other species. The botanical campaigns to identify all trees at the species level started in 2003 and continued every 5 to 6 years. This inventory protocol rasied some mehodological issues given the variability of botanical identification practices along time and among plots. Because vernacular name attributed to trees usually correspond to a variety of botanical species, some taxonomic uncertainty remains whenever a trustworthy botanical name cannot be given thereafter. This taxonomic uncertainty was accounted for in the composition and diversity metrics throught a Bayesian framework estimating the expectancy and variance of composition and diversity metrics. The uncertainty propagation is done by the extrapolation of complete inventories at genus level from real incomplete inventories, on the basis vernacular names replacement. The replacement of vernacular names uses a multinomial distribution Mv([s1, s2, …, sN] ,[α1, α2,…, αN]) based on the observed association probability [α1, α2,…, αN] between each vernacular name v and the species [s1, s2, …, sN] recorded in the inventory. See appendix 1 and Aubry-Kientz et al. (2013) for the detailed methodology.

The functional metrics used a set of 6 functional traits values representing traits of the leaf and wood economics and life history traits. The trait database encompassed 4 traits of the leaves: leaves thickness (L_thick), toughness (L_tough), total chlorophyll content (LCC) and specific leaf area (leaf area per unit dry mass; SLA), 2 traits of the stem: wood specific gravity (SWG) and bark thickness (B_thick) and two life-history traits: maximum specific height (Hmax) and the seed mass (Smass). Traits database came from the BRIDGE project (http://www.ecofog.gf/Bridge/index.html) where traits values were assessed from a selection of individuals located in nine permanent plots in French Guiana, including two in Paracou. To account for intraspecific traits variability, trait values at species level were assessed in sampling one of the measured individuals for iteration rather than averaging at species level. Missing trait values were filled using multivariate imputation by chained equation (mice), restricted to the samples pertaining to the next higher taxonomic level, in order to account for the phylogenetic signal of the functional traits. The dataset comprised 257 botanical species. Whenever a species inventoried was not in the dataset, it was attributed a set of traits values randomly sampled among species of the same next higher taxonomic level. All composition and diversity metrics correspond to the expectancy obtained after 50 iterations of the taxonomic uncertainty propagation framework and the missing trait values filling process.

2.0.3 Composition and diversity metrics

The analysis of taxonomic and functional composition of plots along time was visualized in a two-dimensional ordination space based on non-metric dimensional scaling of succesive floristic or functional inventories. Plots evolution along time was reportedcomparatively to the inventories in 1989, 5 years after disturbance, which correspond to first inventory with a sufficient degree of uncertainty (<30% of undetermined trees). The distancing relative to the reference 1989 inventory was reported using occurrence-based (Jaccard) and abundance-based (Bray-Curtis) similarity measures. The euclidean distance in the two-dimensional ordination space was used to visualize the distancing to reference inventory along time. The functional composition was besides completed by trajectory along time of the community weighted means (CWM) for the 8 leaf, stem and life history traits. To determine whether communities’ assembly deviated from the expectation of a random process we compared the observed similarity patterns to those generated by 50 repetitions of stochastic null models. The null model for taxonomic composition randomly shuffled individuals among plots while preserving the overall species abundance and plots’ tree density. The null model for functional composition produced random traits assemblages by permuting the set of functional traits values among species.

Plots taxonomic diversity corresponded to species Richness and the Hill number translation of Shannon and Simpson indices. These three indices belong to the set of HCDT or generalized entropy, respectively corresponding to the 0, 1 and 2 order of diversity (q). This order q grasps the balance between richness and evenness in the community as it determines the weight of common species in the diversity metric, with an growing emphasis on dominant species as q increases. The functional diversity was reported using the Rao index of quadratic entropy which combines species abundance distribution and average pairwise dissimilarity based on the 6 functional traits specific values.
