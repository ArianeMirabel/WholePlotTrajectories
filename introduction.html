1 Introduction

The large areas covered with tropical forests worldwide hold crucial economic, social and cultural value. They provide not only wood but also multiple non-timber forest products, shelter a diversified fauna, regulate local climate and support carbon, water and many nutrient cycles and ensure cultural and human well-being. The increasing demand for forests products and the resulting shrink of unmanaged areas increase the pressure on the remaining intact forest landscapes as well as threaten the maintenance of their structure, composition and functionning in the long term. The increasing demand for forests products, land-use changes and other human disturbances along with substantial climatic changes make a crucial issue of understanding the response of forests dynamics to such disturbance regimes (Gibson et al. 2011; Morales-Hidalgo, Oswalt, and Somanathan 2015). This has been addressed from the point of view of biomass production and carbon storage which resilience proved subjected to identified cyclic dynamics and therefore gives hope for the maintenance of forest ecosystem and their services. The response of trees biological diversity however has been sparsely studied, although this diversity is a major determinant of forests productivity, stability and functioning as it ensures interspecific complementarity, enhances the use of resources and the store of nutrients, and mitigates the prejudices of diseases and herbivory (Tilman, Isbell, and Cowles 2014). Maintain the integrity of forests functioning thus requires highlighting the impacts and underlying mechanisms of disturbance on trees diversity and question the extent of forests resilience.

Disturbance is defined as rapid changes in the amount and allocation of resources such as variation in the availability of light, the fluxes of heat and water, etc. It influences the environment and competition among species and combineds with environmental filters that select the pool of species fitting the environmental context to shape the composition and structure of communities in the long term: understanding ecosystems requires understanding their disturbance history (White and Jentsch 2001). Although those changes proved impacting the proportion and identity of species, negligible or even positive impacts were highlighted when considering the community as a whole, in accordance with the intermediate disturbance hypothesis (IDH). The IDH that predicts a maximized species diversity at intermediate level of disturbance, however, mainly rely on the study of species richness which gives limited or even misleading information on forests recovery and functioning. The mechanisms under which disturbance shapes communities diversity and structure involve the foster of certain species either stachastically, through the recruitment and dispersal limitation (???) or determinisitcally through the selection of suited ecological types. Such deterministic selection is though to be driven by the trade-off between rapid growth and resources acquisition, therefore “acquisitive strategies” and slow growth with scarce resources []. A way to disentangle the mechanisms of disturbance dynamics would then to identify the shifts in communities functional types definde by the acquisitive or conservative functional traits and by the reproductive strategy through seed and early stage traits (???; Reich 2014; Hérault et al. 2011). Relevant functional types would be defined with the combination of resource acquisition traits: in that respect leaf and stem traits proved relevant in a large litterature (J. Chave et al. 2009; Wright et al. 2004),and life history traits revealing the growth and reproductive strategy (Westoby 1998; Hérault et al. 2011) To have a detailed understanding of forests response to disturbance it is essential to identify the subsequent impact on species identity per se, which underlies any diversity change, as well as on richness, equitability and functional characteristics of the whole community.

Such combination of composition and diversity in browsing all facets of stands assemblage would highlight their resilience to perturbation, and its determinant and underlying ecological processes. Disturbance survey would first assess species turnover and communities convergence through stands taxonomic composition and unveil environmental filters through functional composition that is based on the biological attributes of plants. The set of functional traits chosen for this functional analysis considers functional traits representative of species functional strategy, fitness, and ecology as it is the case for traits of the leaf or stem economics. Composition analysis would be continued with diversity analysis assessing for the whole community the richness and evenness of the taxonomic and functional abundance distribution.
Here we examined the long-term trajectory, in the sense of the evolution along time, of tropical forests taxonomic and functional composition and diversity. We studied the regular inventories over 30 years of twelve Amazonian forest plots, either untouched or disturbed with three disturbance intensity . The inventories were combined with one of the largest functional trait database for tropical trees. We aimed to (i) explicit the impact of disturbance on the long-term trajectory of tropical forest communities composition and diversity, (ii) question the long-term convergence of the composition of communities and (iii) determine their resilience or not, after disturbance.
